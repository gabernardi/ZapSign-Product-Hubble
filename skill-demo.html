Prioritization

How the product team decides what to build next — and what to leave behind.

ZapSign · Product Team Version 1.0 · April 2026
Principles
01
Impact over effort
The question is never "can we build this?" but "should we build this now?"
02
Evidence before conviction
Strong opinions held lightly. Data shifts priorities. Gut feeling does not.
03
Say no to say yes
Every yes to one thing is a no to everything else. Make the trade-off explicit.
04
Reversibility beats perfection
Favor decisions you can undo quickly. Reserve deep analysis for one-way doors.
When to use this framework
Quarterly planning
The team has more ideas than capacity. You need a ranked list backed by evidence.
Focus: outcome alignment and resource fit
Mid-cycle reprioritization
New data arrived. A competitor moved. A deal depends on a feature that wasn't planned.
Focus: cost of switching vs. cost of waiting
Opportunity triage
Sales, CS, or research surfaced a new opportunity. You need to decide fast: now, later, or never.
Focus: signal strength and strategic fit
Compliance mandate
Regulation or contractual obligation. Non-negotiable scope, but timing and approach are yours.
Focus: minimum viable compliance with least friction
Phase 01
Gather and Frame
Collect · Contextualize · Constrain
1
Step 01 2 — 3 days
Build the Opportunity Backlog

Before ranking anything, make sure you have a complete list. Pull from every source: user research, support tickets, sales feedback, strategy bets, and compliance requirements. An incomplete list produces a biased ranking.

Minimum fields per opportunity
OpportunityOne sentence describing what this enables for the user.
SourceWhere did this come from? User research, sales, support, strategy, compliance.
SegmentWhich user segment benefits most? SMB, Mid-Market, Enterprise, All.
EvidenceWhat data supports this? Ticket volume, conversion rate, deal size, NPS verbatim.
ConfidenceHigh, Medium, or Low — how sure are we that this will move the needle?
Signs the backlog is healthy
  • Includes items from at least 3 different sources
  • Every item has traceable evidence, not just opinions
  • The list contains things you will say no to
  • No item is described as a solution — only as an outcome
Common pitfall
  • Collecting only items the loudest voice requested
  • Mixing solutions ("add SSO") with outcomes ("reduce onboarding drop-off")
  • Skipping compliance items because they feel mandatory
  • Starting to rank before the list is complete
Complete opportunity backlog with evidence and confidence levels
2
Step 02 1 — 2 days
Define the Scoring Criteria

A framework is only as good as its axes. Choose 3–4 criteria that reflect what your team values this quarter. Criteria change — and that's fine. What matters is that everyone agrees on them before scoring starts.

User impact
How many users benefit?
Weighted by segment revenue contribution
Strategic alignment
Does it move a company bet?
Mapped to current quarter OKRs
Effort estimate
T-shirt size: S / M / L / XL
Engineering + design + QA combined
Effort scale reference
Small Less than 1 sprint. One squad, no cross-team dependency.
Medium 1–3 sprints. May need design review or API changes.
Large Full quarter or more. Cross-team coordination required.
Criteria are not permanent. Every quarter, revisit the scoring axes. If the company shifts focus from growth to retention, "user impact" might matter less than "churn reduction signal." The framework adapts — you just need to be explicit about it.
Agreed scoring criteria with shared definitions and scale

Transition

Score and Negotiate
Rank · Challenge · Commit
3
Step 03 Half-day workshop
Score Independently, Then Calibrate

Each scorer rates the backlog alone first. Then the team meets to calibrate. The goal is not consensus on every score — it's surfacing disagreements that reveal hidden assumptions.

Solo scoring
Each PM scores independently
Variance check
Flag items with spread > 2
Calibration
Discuss flagged items only
Final ranking
Lock scores, sort by total
Healthy calibration signals
  • Disagreements surface new data nobody had before
  • At least one item moves significantly after discussion
  • The final ranking surprises at least one person
  • Nobody feels their input was ignored
Red flags in calibration
  • One person dominates and others defer without pushback
  • Scores change to avoid conflict, not because of new evidence
  • The final ranking matches exactly what leadership pre-decided
  • Nobody can articulate why item #1 beats item #2
Calibrated priority ranking with documented rationale for top 10
4
Step 04 1 day
Draw the Line

Capacity is finite. Draw an explicit line between what the team will pursue this quarter and what stays in the backlog. The items below the line are not rejected — they are deferred with a reason.

Above the line
  • Committed work that fits within available capacity
  • Each item has an owner and a target quarter
  • Dependencies are mapped and accepted by other teams
Below the line
  • Valuable but not urgent enough given current constraints
  • Revisited next cycle — with fresh data, not memory
  • May move up if capacity opens or evidence strengthens
The line is not a wall. Mid-cycle changes happen. When they do, use the same framework: score the new item, compare it against the current line, and make the trade-off explicit. Never add without removing.

Track and Learn

5
Step 05 Ongoing
Close the Loop on Every Bet

Prioritization without follow-up is just planning theater. Every committed item deserves a retrospective: did it move the metric? Was the effort estimate accurate? What would we do differently? This is what turns prioritization from an event into a system.

Outcome accuracy
Did the bet pay off?
Compare predicted impact vs. actual metric movement
Effort accuracy
Did the estimate hold?
Compare T-shirt size vs. actual sprints consumed
Learning velocity
Are we getting better?
Quarter-over-quarter improvement in prediction accuracy
The goal is calibration, not blame. If half the bets missed their predicted impact, the framework isn't broken — the assumptions feeding it are. Adjust the inputs, not the process.

Output Artifacts

Every prioritization cycle that follows this framework produces these three artifacts.

01
Scored Opportunity Backlog

Complete list of opportunities with evidence, confidence levels, scores, and ranking. The single source of truth for what was considered.

02
Quarterly Commitment Map

The items above the line with owners, target dates, dependencies, and success criteria. What the team promised to deliver.

03
Cycle Retrospective

Outcome accuracy, effort accuracy, and learning velocity for every committed item. The feedback loop that makes the next cycle better.
